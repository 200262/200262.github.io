Playing Defense

Don't let them score, don't give up yardage,
force mistakes and capitalise on them...

While a defense's ultimate goal is to give up the least amount of points possible, they are really trying to limit yardage and create offensive mistakes. Defenses, unlike offenses do not have to be set at the snap, meaning they can attempt to create confusion and disguise what they are trying to do. Defenses will either have a base 3-4 formation or 4-3 formation. These first number represents the number of defensive lineman, while the second represents the number of linebackers. The two systems require fundamentally different kinds of players, and while 4-3 is more prevalent, many teams are starting to shift to a 3-4 system. A 3-4 defense presents more creative ways to blitz and cover than a traditional 4-3 might allow for. The major difference is that a 3-4 will struggle to stop the run without a good nose tackle, the guy who starts across from the center on defense. A 3-4 defense places a premium on good pass rushing outside linebackers and do-it-all inside linebackers. The 4-3 places more of a premium on the defensive line.

In their base formations, both systems have four defensive backs, two cornerbacks and two safeties. A defense may choose to subsitute a linebacker out for an extra defensive back in attempt to stop a more-pass happy attack, or stop an offense in a passing situation. Having one extra defensive back is called a nickel formation and having two is called the dime formation. In the dime formation, teams may choose to blitz one of their defensive backs in an attempt to catch the offense off guard. In situations where the defense knows the offense needs a long pass (such as a Hail Mary), they may put seven or eight defensive backs on the field to stop the very deep pass and give up time-wasting runs.
